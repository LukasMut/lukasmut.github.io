Lukas

Investigating brains and machines

PhD student in Computational Neuroscience

I am a first-year PhD student in Computational Neuroscience at the Max-Planck-Institute (MPI) for Human Cognitive and Brain Sciences. I decipher the semantic representations of objects in the human brain and in artificial neural networks. I am mainly advised by Martin Hebart. Previously, I was an MSc student of Isabelle Augenstein, Johannes Bjerva, and Maria Barrett , mainly supervised by Johannes. Prior to this, I was an undergraduate student in Psychology at the University of Vienna. There, I worked as a Research Assistant in the field of Cognitive Psychology in the groups of Ulrich Ansorge and Claus Lamm, mostly supervised by Thomas Ditye. During this time, I've interned in the Clinical Neuropsychology research group at LMU Munich. I also have an undergraduate degree in Political Science & Philosophy.

My main interests lie in what Machine Learning can learn from Neuroscience, and vice versa, how the Neurosciences can benefit from Machine Learning models. Thus, I revel in analysing cognitive data, building neural networks, and decode the hidden representations of these models to foster a more comprehensive understanding. Mostly, however, I am debugging, deciphering model behaviour, using print statements, and reflecting on why things are not working. Have a look at my CV, the projects or publication section for more information about my work and interests. Feel free to reach out to me, if you believe our research intentions are aligned.
