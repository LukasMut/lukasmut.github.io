Lukas

Investigating brains and machines

PhD student in Computational Cognitive Neuroscience

I am a first-year PhD student in Computational Cognitive Neuroscience (CCN) at the Max-Planck-Institute (MPI) for Human Cognitive and Brain Sciences. I decipher the semantic representations of objects in deep neural networks (DNNs) and make them more human-interpretable. I am mainly advised by Martin Hebart. Previously, I was a MSc student in IT & Cognition / Computer Science of Isabelle Augenstein, Johannes Bjerva, and Maria Barrett, mainly supervised by Johannes. Prior to this, I was an undergraduate student in Psychology at the University of Vienna. There, I worked as a Research Assistant in the field of Cognitive Psychology in the groups of Ulrich Ansorge and Claus Lamm, mostly supervised by Thomas Ditye. During this time, I've interned in the Clinical Neuropsychology research group at LMU Munich. I also have an undergraduate degree in Political Science & Philosophy.

My main interests lie in the intersection of Machine Learning and Neuroscience. In particular, I am interested in how computational models can be inspired by the processing mechanisms of the human brain. Thus, I revel in analysing cognitive data, building neural networks, and decode the hidden representations of these models to foster a more comprehensive understanding. Mostly, however, I am debugging, examining model behaviour, using print statements, and contemplating why things are not working. Have a look at my CV, the projects or publications section for more information about my work and interests. Feel free to reach out to me, if you believe our research intentions are aligned.
