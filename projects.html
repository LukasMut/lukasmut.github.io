Current Projects

cognitively inspired NLP

Investigating with Nora Hollenstein and Maria Barrett how different reading tasks, and hence their respective cognitive loads, affect human attention, and neural networks in solving NLP tasks. In this endeavour, we are exploiting the recently developed ZuCo corpus. Code will soon be available on GitHub.

subjective Question Answering (QA)

Humans ask Google (and other search engines) a variety of different questions. For the most part, those questions are subjective in nature. People want to know where to find things such as a romantic hotel or a great techno club (I know, those two things are quite different, or maybe not - I guess, that's subjective too). Thus, it's important that algorithms are capable of accurately answering not only factual but also subjective questions. Together with Johannes Bjerva and Isabelle Augestein I am working on a project related to solving this conundrum, where I am currently looking into NL2SQL algorithms. Code will soon be available on GitHub.

Previous Projects

Emoji Prediction

What's the last emoji? The use of emojis is more frequent than ever due to both the rapid growth of social media platforms and the increasing amount of available emojis in the virtual world. That's why, Gordon and I thought it would be a fun task to predict emojis in Twitter tweets given the words people use in their tweets. Code is available here.

Cross-Domain Authorship Attribution

Authorship Attribution is the task of determining the author of a text from a set of candidates. This so-called classification task (multi-class at most times) is highly relevant for fields such as forensics or the detection of plagiarism. Here, we were classifying authors of fan-fictional texts such as J.K. Rowling copycats. Read our paper here and look at the code here.
